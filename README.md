Every civilization that ever collapsed kept careful records of its own decline. Nearly all of them misread the records.
The Samsara Method: Complete Works is a field manual for reading them correctly. The premise is stark. 
Everything a society does runs on gradients: differences in energy, information, and value that hold order against entropy. 
Civilizations are machines for managing gradients. When the gradients steepen faster than the machine can flatten them, the wheel turns.
The framework rests on six Pillars (Energy, Information, Product, Service, Transport, Entertainment) and one uncomfortable theorem: a system is never as strong as its average. 
It is exactly as strong as its weakest Pillar. From that floor the book builds a complete analytical apparatus. Seven structural axioms. 
An eight-step diagnostic method. Four failure modes that exhaust the ways institutions betray themselves: mismanagement, malfeasance, rent-seeking, incompetence. 
A quantitative engine that turns the architecture into measurable indices, cascade thresholds, and trajectory projections. 
The lens works at every scale, from a household to a superpower, because the mathematics of sandpiles does not care how big the sandpile is.
Then the Complete Works goes where most systems theory refuses to. Beneath the Pillars, into substrate: the religions, ideologies, 
and identities that actually purchase cooperation at scale, and the biosphere that silently underwrites everything else. 
Into war, reading Clausewitz through the gradient lens as politics' violent bookkeeping. And against the hard cases: Ukraine, Mariupol, Nagorno-Karabakh, Afghanistan, the contemporary United States.
What makes the volume unusual is its discipline. Seven binding rules govern every analysis, including the requirement that the analyst turn the four failure modes on their own work. 
This is a framework that audits itself, because the first system any method must diagnose honestly is the analyst.
The wheel turns. Gradients drive it.
